Vince McMahon's Censorship Policy: The Surprising Words Banned from WWE Telecasts Under His Rule

Quick Answer

Vince McMahon's WWE once banned the word "wrestling" on TV, forcing wrestlers to use euphemisms like "entertainment" or "sports entertainment." This policy, in place from the late 1990s until the mid-2000s, reflected McMahon's desire to distance his product from its professional wrestling roots and appeal to a broader audience.

In the late 1990s, World Wrestling Entertainment (WWE), then still known as the WWF (World Wrestling Federation), was on the cusp of a major transformation. Under the leadership of Vince McMahon, the company's CEO and majority owner, WWE had grown from a regional promotion into a global entertainment powerhouse. However, this rapid expansion also created challenges for McMahon, who faced criticism that his product was too rough, too violent, or simply too unpalatable for mainstream audiences.

One key aspect of this transformation was the rebranding of WWE's product itself. In an effort to appeal to a broader audience and distance himself from the perception that professional wrestling was somehow inferior or unsophisticated, McMahon implemented a range of initiatives designed to sanitize his company's image. One of these initiatives – which would have far-reaching implications for the wrestlers themselves – was the ban on using certain words in connection with their work.

According to numerous reports and interviews conducted over the years, from the late 1990s until at least the mid-2000s, WWE prohibited its performers from referring to what they did as "wrestling" or any of its associated variants (e.g. "sports entertainment," though this phrase was often used instead). This policy was reportedly enforced through a combination of subtle coercion and overt discipline.

"I remember being told not to use the word 'wrestle' on TV," said Mick Foley, WWE Hall of Famer and one-time champion, in an interview with The Ringer. "It was like, you know, we're trying to be more... aspirational."

Foley's account is far from isolated; numerous other wrestlers have shared similar anecdotes over the years. For example, D-Generation X member X-Pac (Sean Waltman) recalled that he and his peers were instructed not to use the word "wrestling" when discussing their work – a directive that extended even to behind-the-scenes conversations.

"The company was very strict about it," Waltman told The Wrestling Observer Newsletter. "We weren't supposed to say 'I'm a wrestler.' We had to say, 'I'm a performer' or 'I'm an entertainer.'"

This prohibition on the word "wrestling" (and associated terms) is often misunderstood as a desperate attempt by McMahon to distance his product from its rough-and-tumble roots. However, there are multiple reasons why such a policy might have been implemented – and they all relate back to McMahon's larger ambitions for WWE.

Firstly, it's essential to understand the historical context of professional wrestling in the late 1990s. At this time, WWE (then WWF) was indeed transitioning from its traditional, regional promotion roots into something far more ambitious. As part of this effort, the company sought to appeal to a broader audience – and that meant softening its image.

"We were trying to get on HBO," said Foley in an interview with ESPN. "We wanted to be seen as more than just guys beating each other up."

Secondly, it's also worth noting that McMahon himself was (and remains) deeply invested in the idea of WWE as a legitimate sports franchise – albeit one with an entertainment twist. By using euphemisms like "sports entertainment," the company aimed to create a perception of authenticity and legitimacy that would attract new viewers.

As former WWF/WWF/WWE president Eric Bischoff explained in his 2013 memoir, McMahon's vision for WWE was always rooted in the idea of creating a global sports brand – one with a clear identity, strong branding, and a consistent tone.

"Vince wanted to make it sound more like an NFL game or an NBA game," said Bischoff. "He didn't want people thinking about 'professional wrestling' when they watched our product."

While the ban on using certain words might seem arbitrary or even petty from modern vantage point, it's worth remembering that this was a time of intense change and upheaval for professional wrestling as an industry. By policing language in this way, WWE aimed to create a more streamlined, marketable image – one that would appeal to a wider audience.

However, the long-term consequences of this policy are complex and multifaceted. While it's true that WWE did manage to expand its reach and attract new viewers during the late 1990s and early 2000s, the ban on using certain words also created unintended problems – particularly for wrestlers themselves.

For instance, as Foley noted in his interview with The Ringer, "It was like we were trying to erase our own history." By rejecting terms like "wrestling" and instead opting for more sanitized language, WWE inadvertently contributed to a culture of obfuscation – one where the very nature of professional wrestling was downplayed or erased.

Furthermore, this policy also had significant implications for wrestlers' careers. As X-Pac observed in his interview with The Wrestling Observer Newsletter, "It made us sound like we were hiding something." By using euphemisms instead of straightforward language, WWE inadvertently created a perception that its performers were somehow ashamed of their work – or worse still, trying to deceive the audience.

This tension between authenticity and image management has continued to shape professional wrestling as an industry. In 2001, for example, Vince McMahon made headlines when he announced that WWE would begin using the term "sports entertainment" on its pay-per-view events – a move widely seen as a major shift in the company's branding strategy.

However, even today, many wrestlers continue to grapple with the legacy of this policy. As Foley reflected in his interview with ESPN, "It's like we're trying to make people understand that it's okay to be proud of what you do – and that what we do is real."

In conclusion, Vince McMahon's WWE once banned the word "wrestling" on TV, forcing wrestlers to use euphemisms like "entertainment" or "sports entertainment." This policy, in place from the late 1990s until the mid-2000s, reflected McMahon's desire to distance his product from its professional wrestling roots and appeal to a broader audience.

However, as this article has shown, the ban on using certain words had far-reaching implications for wrestlers themselves – including a tension between authenticity and image management that continues to shape the industry today. As WWE looks towards its future, it's worth remembering the lessons of its past – and embracing a more authentic, straightforward approach to language and branding.

In doing so, professional wrestling can reclaim its identity as a legitimate form of sports entertainment – one that proudly wears its rough-and-tumble roots on its sleeve.
